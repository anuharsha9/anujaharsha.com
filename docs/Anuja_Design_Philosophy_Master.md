I make complex technical systems **obvious** — as obvious as knowing to sit in a chair — without dumbing them down. Everything below is in service of that.

## 1. Origin — I knew at 15

I didn't fall into design. I didn't get bored of engineering at 25 and pivot. I knew at 15 and never looked back.

- **2009, age 15:** A career-counseling workshop. The instructor wrote *Interest + Talent = Career* on the board, and it hit me like a light bulb. I'd spent a decade in art classes since age 5 — charcoal, watercolor, oil pastels, embroidery — and I was the kid who was bizarrely good with computers (we had a desktop from when I was in 2nd grade, rare in early-2000s India; I learned CorelDRAW in 5th grade). Art + computers = design. That was it.
- **2010, age ~16:** Finished 10th grade, joined an animation institute. Morning junior college, evening animation classes. 2D animation, frame-by-frame, Photoshop, Maya. Running on adrenaline and over-sweet black coffee, loving every second.
- **2011, age 16–17:** Discovered **UI** for the first time — Apple GUI kits, icons, layout grids. Something clicked. Unlike everything else, it didn't feel like effort. It felt like oxygen. Won my first logo competition (₹1000 — my "oh, this is real" moment).
- **2012, age 17 (August, ~6 weeks before my 18th birthday):** First official job — a 12-person mobile-app startup, two co-founders, a handful of engineers, and me. They took me seriously and I took the work seriously. I did everything: mockups, branding, logos, motion, UI. That place was my bootcamp.
- By **21** I had two bachelor's degrees, four years of experience, a grade-4 violin diploma, and a husband.

I didn't *find* design. I grew up with it. It became the backbone of who I am — and maybe that's why I've survived legacy systems, 40-year-old products, engineer-led teams, ML workflows, startups, motherhood, and layoffs. There's a real power in that kind of clarity. It becomes part of your spine.

**The timeline, locked:** knew at 15 (2009), started designing at 16 (2010), professional since 2012 (13+ years).

## 2. Identity — who I actually am

I'm a Senior Product Designer by title, but the real work is **obsessing over how humans experience things.** I'm a user-experience specialist who happens to carry a product-design title.

- 13+ years in, started at 16. At this point UX is **instinct**, not process — I *feel* when something's wrong before I can name it.
- I don't just design — I **build.** The full loop: feel the problem → design the solution → ship the code. I prototype in real code because the biggest risk usually isn't the visuals, it's the logic.
- **B2B / enterprise is my home** — data-heavy, technical, the kind of product most designers find intimidating or boring. That's exactly where I do my best work.
- **No formal CS background**, but I genuinely want to understand the systems I'm designing for — and I go learn them, deeply.
- **AI-era stance:** In a world of AI, human experience matters *more*, not less — because AI is built for humans. The more powerful the technology, the more the experience layer matters. AI doesn't replace experience design; it amplifies it. (It also removed my one bottleneck — the raw hours of hand-coding between my ideas and shipped reality. The judgment was always mine; now it moves at the speed of my thinking.)

## 3. The Chair Philosophy — my definition of UX

The chair isn't a cute analogy. It's how I define UX and my default lens on the world.

> When you look at a chair, the only thing that comes to mind is to sit on it.

That's it. The design is so good you don't *think* about the design — you just do the thing. No instructions, no onboarding, no tutorial. You look, you sit.

Everything in the world is either designed so well you **don't notice it** (you just use it) or so badly you **can't stop noticing** (frustration). A door handle, a water bottle, an enterprise BI tool — I judge them all the same way: *is it as obvious as knowing to sit in a chair?*

The whole point of making anything is to reach that obviousness. And you get there by obsessing over how humans experience things. That's the job.

## 4. The 5 Affordance Laws — the chair test, made operational

The chair test, turned into five laws you can name in a design review ("that fails law 3"). Bake them into the primitives so obviousness is **structural and inherited for free** — never re-applied screen by screen.

1. **Prominence = importance.** The thing that matters most is unmistakably the biggest. Never make the user hunt for the headline.
2. **State before words.** On-track / off-track, gain / loss, asset / debt is readable by **color + sign + weight** *before* you read the label.
3. **Fact ≠ forecast.** A current balance looks settled and exact; a projection looks uncertain (range/cone, lighter). A forecast must never masquerade as a fact. *(This is "real data, no assumptions" made visual.)*
4. **Actionable looks actionable.** Tappable / editable / "ask" elements invite the action; static info stays quiet.
5. **One element, one job.** No Swiss-army cards.

## 5. The pattern in everything I build

When I look at my work — professional and personal — it's all the same kind of thing: **adaptive systems that learn the user and help them decide or improve.**

A scheduling engine made legible. An ML platform nobody could use, turned into one they relied on. A natural-language layer over data so people could just *ask*. And on my own time: a forecasting engine for my finances, an authorization system for AI agents, a writing coach that learns my style, a cooking companion that learns how I cook, a college-and-career path builder.

I didn't plan that pattern — it's just how my brain works. I can't build the boring version of anything. The moment I touch a problem, I'm asking "how could this learn? how could this adapt? how could this make the person better at the thing?" An archive for my articles became an AI writing coach because I literally can't help it.

So if I have a point of view, it's this: **software should learn you and extend your thinking.**

## 6. What I'm drawn to

I run toward the hard problem nobody wants to touch — the 40-year-old black box with no documentation, the powerful system nobody can actually use, the mess everyone's avoiding.

I get genuinely obsessed. The less I can find, the more I want to find. I'll build the architecture from scratch, sit in support calls, ask hundreds of questions, befriend the one engineer who remembers how it works — until I understand it better than almost anyone who didn't write the code. You can't rebuild something beautifully until you understand the mess.

And I'm a **utility person, not an entertainment person.** I build things people *need*, not things that just engage them. Productivity over novelty. If it doesn't solve a real problem in someone's actual life, I'm not interested. I only build what's missing — never what already exists.

## 7. How I work

- **I befriend engineering and I listen. A lot.** I don't throw designs over a wall. I get into the technical layer, learn the constraints, understand what's actually buildable. Engineers trust me because I do the work to understand their world — and that trust is what gets good design shipped.
- **I build the map when there isn't one.** No docs? I'll create them. No process? I'll make one. I don't wait for tickets or for someone to hand me clarity. I go find it and keep the work moving. (At one point my PM wrote the Jira tickets *after* seeing my mockups.)
- **I back my design with evidence, not taste.** Design instincts don't mean anything if you can't back them with logic or evidence. When I disagree with an engineer or a PM, I don't pull rank — I make the case with user-comprehension logic, and we land on the better answer. Usually the rejected versions made the final one stronger.
- **Product comes first. Always. Zero ego.** I don't think about myself when I'm working — I think about the product. I'll vent privately when it's hard, but it never touches the work.
- **I build what I actually need.** Every product I've made on my own came from a real problem I had. And because I have the chops, they come out good enough that other people want them too. That's the whole secret — I'm not showing off. I'm solving my own problems with real craft, and the impressive part is a side effect.

## 8. Aesthetic — aurora, ocean, dim light

This is innate personal taste, not a trend. I've loved the northern lights for as long as I can remember, and I try to get waves into my work somehow — my love for the ocean and dark, dim lighting is just who I am.

| Source | What it means in the work |
|---|---|
| **Northern lights (aurora)** | Curtains of light that ripple — horizontal bands that drift and breathe. Subtle, alive, ethereal. |
| **Ocean waves** | The movement pattern everywhere — slow, organic, continuous. Never abrupt. |
| **Dark, dim lighting** | Not "dark mode for looks." Genuinely preferred — ocean at night, lit from below, barely there. |
| **Teal accent** | The color of deep ocean + aurora. It *is* the system, not decoration. |

**Intensity = "whispering intensity."** The screen is alive, but barely — you're not sure if it's moving. The base color itself can breathe between pure black and faint navy.

**The ocean-boat metaphor (governs scroll):** the user is floating on a boat. The fixed aurora background is the ocean — always present, never scrolling. Content zones are the scenery drifting into view: they blur in, hold steady so you can observe them, then blur away as new scenery appears. Serene, continuous, breathing — never snappy or jarring.

## 9. Voice & tone — Apple-style minimalist

Simple, straightforward, direct, confident — never boastful.

**Do:** Lead with outcomes and specifics (numbers, before/after, what shipped). Short sentences; fragments are fine. Let whitespace do the work. Use my raw voice, not AI filler.

**Avoid:** Flowery language ("crafting digital experiences," "passionate about…"). Filler adjectives (innovative, cutting-edge, world-class). Marketing-speak that says nothing.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "Connecting the structural integrity of code with the emotional resonance of design." |
| **Good** | "I prototype in code because the biggest risk isn't the visuals — it's the logic." |
| **Bad** | "Passionate designer creating human-centered solutions." |
| **Good** | "Senior Product Designer. Enterprise systems. AI-augmented workflow." |

The "dad test": a layperson should get the context with zero jargon — while still speaking to recruiters and design leaders (strategy, not just UI). If the visual already shows it, the words shouldn't repeat it.

## 10. What I believe about design

- Complexity isn't the enemy. *Illegible* complexity is. The job is clarity, not removal.
- The best products come from solving a real problem you actually have — not from guessing at a market.
- Don't build what already exists. Build what's missing.
- Design that engineering can't build isn't good design. Feasibility is part of the craft.
- A small, surgical fix that cuts real cognitive load is worth as much as a big redesign.
- You earn the right to a strong opinion by understanding the system better than anyone expected you to.
- **Never a loose end.** Every path must resolve into a conclusive, satisfying, actionable next step — never a dangling question with no way forward. I hit this constantly in enterprise platforms and it genuinely bothers me as a UX enthusiast: every flow, every dead-end, every empty state owes the user a way to finish.

## 12. How I want to be worked *with*

- **Plan before execute.** For any creative or structural change, present a plan and get approval before writing code. No surprises.
- **Teach as you go.** Don't blindly code — narrate the *why*, surface tradeoffs, explain bugs and fixes in plain language with B2B/enterprise analogies. No jargon dumps.
- **Talk in prose, not menus.** I reframe questions (turn a 3-way fork into a destination with interim paths). Multiple-choice flattens that. Reserve menus for genuinely binary/tooling choices.
- **Real data, no assumptions.** Every model or projection uses real, sourced numbers — never invented. For finance work: model as a band/range with a floor and ceiling, never a single confident line.
- **UX substance > visual polish.** Get the flows and decision-support right. Visuals should be appealing, not dull — but pixel-perfect gold-plating isn't the goal. Judge a change by whether it helps the user decide and act.
- **Rewrite over patch.** No patchwork. If a file gets messy (>3 edits), do a clean rewrite.
- **Document everything.** Plans, decisions, refactors get written down so history persists.

## 13. The build layer — tokens are law
*(Operational, for codebase work; omit when adapting this doc for interview or public use.)*

- **No hardcoded values, ever.** Colors, type, spacing, radii, shadows, easings, durations — all from tokens. Enforced, not aspirational.
- **One color per hue.** Main palette: black, white, zinc greys, teal accent. Semantic colors (emerald, blue, rose, orange, purple, cyan, pink, navy) are for animation and data-viz only — not UI chrome.
- **Motion is token-based and water-like.** NO crossfades or opacity fades — ever. Use **blur-to-focus** (entering) and **focus-to-blur** (leaving) everywhere. Content stays sharp ~76% of scroll (desktop) / ~80% (mobile) so it can be *read* before it dissolves. Gentle parallax (~30px desktop / 16px mobile), no snaps. Particles: flat hard-edged teal/white dots, spring-formed then frozen — no glow, no ambient drift.
- **Accessibility built in.** Text tokens carry WCAG ratios; teal focus ring is a global token; `prefers-reduced-motion` kills animation globally.
- **Locked experiences — do not touch:** Brain SVG ("Explore My Mind"), HeroAurora render code, case-study trailers (RC/ML/DSML).
