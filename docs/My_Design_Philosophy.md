# My Design Approach, Identity & Philosophy

*A working document — who I am as a designer, how I work, and what I believe. Written plainly, the way I'd actually say it.*

---

## The one line

I make complex technical systems legible — without dumbing them down. That's the whole thing. Everything else is a footnote to that.

---

## What I actually do

Most of my career has been in B2B enterprise — data-heavy, technical, the kind of products most designers find intimidating or boring. That's exactly where I do my best work. I take something tangled, undocumented, and intimidating, and I make it usable for the people who live in it all day — admins, analysts, engineers, data scientists. Power users.

I don't simplify by removing the sophistication. I make the sophistication *readable*. There's a difference, and it's the difference between "making it dumb" and "making it clear." A data scientist once asked me if I was trying to make his work dumb. I wasn't. I was making it legible. That distinction is basically my entire job.

---

## What I'm drawn to

I run toward the hard problem nobody wants to touch. The 40-year-old black box with no documentation. The powerful system nobody can actually use. The thing everyone's avoiding because it's a mess.

I get genuinely obsessed. The less I can find, the more I want to find. I'll build the product's whole architecture from scratch, sit in support calls, ask hundreds of questions, befriend the one engineer who remembers how it works — until I understand it better than almost anyone who didn't write the code. I want to understand it *completely*, because you can't rebuild something beautifully until you actually understand the mess.

I'm a utility person, not an entertainment person. I build things people *need*, not things that just engage them. Productivity over novelty. If it doesn't solve a real problem in someone's actual life, I'm not interested.

---

## The pattern in everything I build

When I look at my work — professional and personal — it's all the same kind of thing: **adaptive systems that learn the user and help them decide or improve.**

A scheduling engine made legible. An ML platform nobody could use, turned into something they could. A natural-language layer over data so people could just *ask*. And on my own time: a forecasting engine for my finances, an authorization system for AI agents, a writing coach that learns my style, a cooking companion that learns how I cook, a college-and-career path builder.

I didn't plan that pattern. It's just how my brain works. I can't build the boring version of anything. The moment I touch a problem, I'm asking "how could this learn? how could this adapt? how could this make the person better at the thing?" An archive for my articles became an AI writing coach because I literally can't help it.

That's my point of view, if I have one: **software should learn you and extend your thinking.**

---

## How I work

**I befriend engineering and I listen. A lot.** I don't throw designs over a wall. I get into the technical layer, learn the constraints, understand what's actually buildable. I close the gap between design and engineering because I've stood on both sides of it. Engineers trust me because I do the work to understand their world — and that trust is what gets good design shipped.

**I build the map when there isn't one.** No documentation? I'll create it. No process? I'll make one. I don't wait for tickets or for someone to hand me clarity. I go find it, and I keep the work moving.

**I back my design with evidence, not taste.** Design instincts don't mean anything if you can't back them with logic or evidence. When I disagree with an engineer or a PM, I don't pull rank — I make the case with user-comprehension logic, and we land on the better answer. Usually the rejected versions made the final one stronger.

**Product comes first. Always. Zero ego.** I genuinely don't think about myself when I'm working — I think about the product. I'll vent privately when it's hard, but it never touches the work. The work gets my clearest thinking regardless of how I feel that day.

**I build what I actually need.** Every product I've made on my own came from a real problem I had. And because I have the chops, they come out good enough that other people want them too. That's the whole secret — I'm not trying to show off. I'm just solving my own problems with real craft, and the impressive part is a side effect.

---

## What I believe about design

- Complexity isn't the enemy. *Illegible* complexity is. The job is clarity, not removal.
- The best products come from solving a real problem you actually have — not from guessing at a market.
- Don't build what already exists. Build what's missing.
- Design that engineering can't build isn't good design. Feasibility is part of the craft.
- A small, surgical fix that reduces real cognitive load is worth as much as a big redesign.
- You earn the right to a strong opinion by understanding the system better than anyone expected you to.

---

## How I operate (beyond design)

I always have a plan — and I'm always willing to adjust it when life throws a curveball. Clear about the goal, flexible about the path. I don't white-knuckle a single plan and break when reality interferes; I hold the destination firm and let the route flex. That's how I've moved through everything — a career across two countries, legacy systems, restructurings, building a life from very little. Clarity plus adaptability. It's the most durable way I know to operate.

Made systems that customers couldn't use into systems they relied on. WebFOCUS was named a Customer Experience Leader in Dresner's 2025 awards during that period.

**On my own:** Built and shipped five live, working AI products — a predictive wealth engine, an agent-authorization platform (Warden), a writing coach (Inkwell), a college-and-career builder (Pathwise), and a voice-first, local-first cooking companion (published, public). All built for myself, to solve my own problems. All proof of the same philosophy.
