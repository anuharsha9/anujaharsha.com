## The Chair Philosophy — Core Identity

The chair metaphor isn't just a cute analogy. It's Anu's **definition of UX**. It's how she sees the world.

> "When you look at a chair... the only thing that comes to mind is to sit on it."

That's it. That's user experience. The design is so good, you don't think about the design. You just DO the thing. The chair doesn't need instructions. It doesn't need onboarding. It doesn't need a tutorial. You look at it. You sit.

**The water bottle extension:**
> "You know a water bottle. If the mouth isn't easy to drink — what's the point of the water bottle? Right? I can't drink properly, or it spills, or too less water coming, you know? And the grip? If it's too fat to hold or too thin to not carry enough water — what's the point?"

Everything in the world is either:
- Designed so well you don't notice → you just USE it
- Designed so badly you can't stop noticing → frustration

Anu lives in this space. It's not a job. It's a default lens. EVERYTHING gets evaluated this way. A door handle. A water bottle. An enterprise BI tool. The bar is always: **is it as obvious as knowing to sit in a chair?**

## Identity: Not a "Designer-Designer"

> "I don't see myself as a designer-designer. I'm a user experience specialist hiding behind the title of Senior Product Designer."

The title is the industry label. The real work is: **obsessing over how humans experience things.** Making complex systems feel obvious. Making enterprise software feel as natural as sitting in a chair.

## The AI-Era Stance

> "In this world of AI — the human user experience will ALWAYS matter. Because AI is designed for Humans."

This is Anu's stance in 2026. Everyone's talking about AI replacing things. Anu's position:
- AI doesn't eliminate the need for human experience design — it **amplifies** it
- AI is built FOR humans → the human interface is the entire point
- The more powerful the technology, the more important the experience layer becomes

## Instinct + Craft — The 13-Year Compound Effect

> "When you think about humans and their experience with digital products — it doesn't matter whether you know how to incorporate AI into the workflow. For someone whose default lens is UX and has been designing since the raw age of 16 — design decisions at this point are about INSTINCT because I've worked for so long. Can you imagine how easy it is for me to design any interface? And then to add the vibe-coding skills as cherry on top."

Key insight: After 13 years, UX isn't a process for Anu — it's **instinct**. She doesn't follow a framework. She *feels* when something is wrong. The chair test isn't something she applies — it's automatic.

And vibe-coding means she doesn't just design — she **builds**. The full loop: feel the problem → design the solution → ship the code.

### "Obsessing over how humans experience things"

This isn't a job description. It's a compulsion. She can't walk past a bad door handle without noticing. She can't use a bad app without mentally redesigning it. She's been doing this since she was 16.

### Key insights from brainstorming:
- "Effortless" was too generic — a buzzword that doesn't carry weight

- The word "designer" doesn't fully represent her — "user experience" is more accurate
- The AI-era angle is powerful positioning for 2026 job market
- Whatever the line is, it should bridge to the bio: → "Hi, I'm Anuja"

### Current favorites:
1. **"That's why I design."** — simple, warm, motivational
2. **"The human experience will always matter."** — bold stance, AI-era relevant
3. **"That's why user experience will always matter."** — connects chair to career
4. **"I obsess over how humans experience things."** — raw, personal, avoids "designer"

## Innate Aesthetic Sensibilities — Ocean, Aurora, Dim Light

> "I've loved the northern lights for as long as I can imagine. I always try to incorporate waves somehow in my design — my love for ocean and dark dim lighting is innate and much preferred."

This is a deep personal taste, not a trend:

- **Northern lights (aurora borealis)** — curtains of light that ripple. Not a glow, not a blob. Horizontal bands that drift and breathe. Subtle, alive, ethereal.
- **Ocean waves** — the movement pattern that shows up everywhere in her work. Slow, organic, continuous. Never abrupt.
- **Dark dim lighting** — not dark-mode-for-aesthetic. Genuinely preferred atmosphere. Like ocean at night, lit from below. Barely there.
- **Teal as the accent** — this isn't arbitrary. It's the color of deep ocean + aurora. It is the design system, not decoration.

### What this means for the portfolio:
| Element | Translation |
|---|---|
| **Hero background** | CSS aurora — teal curtains drifting across near-black, 12-20s cycles. Not a glow. Not orbs. Waves. |
| **Motion language** | Everything moves like water — blur-to-focus, slow dissolves, no snaps |
| **Intensity** | "Whispering Intensity" — the screen is alive but barely. You're not sure if it's moving |
| **Color temperature** | The base color of the screen itself should breathe between pure black and faint navy |
