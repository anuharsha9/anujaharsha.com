## Option B — Structured Raw Story

I got into ML Functions through a much smaller ML explainability project. A data scientist showed me a preview of an explainability popup from another software and basically said, I want this in WebFOCUS. That could have easily turned into a surface-level UI task, but I didn’t do it that way. I went digging. I asked a lot of questions. I wanted to understand why the popup existed, what part of the workflow it belonged to, what happened before it, what happened after it, and how it fit into Reporting Server, Data Flow, Run Model, and the Hub. I was brand new to the company, still learning the design system, but that didn’t stop me. If anything, it made me more curious.

That explainability project is really what earned me ML Functions. I nailed the explainability work, and the data scientist appreciated the fact that I wasn’t designing it blindly. He had worked with other designers before who apparently hadn’t taken the time to really understand the workflow. I did. I asked questions, I dug in, I tried to understand the whole thing before touching the design. That earned trust. Around the same time, I had also realized that the overall machine learning workflow itself was not good. I had seen enough to know this whole experience needed a redesign. So the full ML Functions revamp happened because a few things aligned at once: the VP wanted better output, the PM agreed something needed work, the data scientist trusted me, my design director supported it, and I was already pushing the idea that the workflow needed to be redone.

When I first opened the old ML workflow, my reaction was simple: it was really shitty. The actual act of getting into it was exhausting. You had to go through the plus menu, into data flow, into a new browser tab, drag a dataset into the Data Flow canvas, go to Train Models, drag a model type like binary classification or regression onto the canvas, and then deal with a split-screen layout that looked like an error state before you had even really started. There was a red exclamation mark and a message about no results being generated yet. It looked like failure even though it was really just telling you to click play and start training. The whole thing was buried, click-heavy, fragmented, and visually misleading. It was also powerful, which made it worse in a way, because something genuinely useful had been wrapped in a workflow that felt hostile.

What bothered me wasn’t just the number of clicks. It was that the whole thing seemed to assume the user already knew where they were, what the system expected, and what each weird visual cue meant. The old workflow asked too much of the user too early. Even the hyperparameter configuration was painful. To get there, you had to dig through a cascading right-click menu, sometimes through multiple layers, then open one parameter at a time in separate popups. If you wanted to configure all the hyperparameters, you were repeating the same painful drill over and over. There was apparently another path somewhere in the UI, but it wasn’t obvious. And if a shorter path is hidden, it doesn’t really count.

The workflow lived inside Reporting Server, and that mattered. Reporting Server’s Data Flow canvas was one of the most powerful parts of WebFOCUS. You could drag in datasets, merge them, manipulate them, assess quality, profile data, apply operations, configure pills, and basically do all kinds of data prep and transformation. That was important context because it explained why ML had become so entangled with Data Flow in the first place. But once the data was ready for ML, having the entire Data Flow canvas visible all the time didn’t make much sense. Machine learning should have been about the selected dataset and the model workflow, not about forcing users to stare at the whole upstream machinery while trying to train a model. That was one of the biggest mental shifts for me: Data Flow was important upstream, but it didn’t need to dominate the training experience itself.

Emotionally, I wasn’t sitting there going wow, what an exciting ML opportunity. Broken workflows don’t inspire me that way. My natural instinct is to fix things. I get irritated by bad UX. I get obsessed. The intimidation was real because WebFOCUS was a huge legacy product and I had never worked in a corporate environment like that before, but intimidation did not stop me. It was more like: okay, this is bad, so let’s fix it. That was the mindset.

The fact that I had zero ML background didn’t really scare me. I’ve always believed that if you’re determined enough, you can learn whatever you need to learn in order to solve the problem in front of you. ML was just another example of that. I asked Marcus questions. I asked AI questions. I researched binary classification, regression, random forest, trees, neural networks, time series, whatever I needed. Eventually that pushed me into taking an MIT course on AI and ML for product design. I paid for that myself because I felt like the work had outgrown surface-level understanding. I wanted foundational literacy so I could speak more credibly with the data scientist and make better design decisions. The course was mixed. Some parts were too technical for me, especially the math and coding-heavy bits. But it gave me enough of the mechanics and terminology behind machine learning that I could understand what was happening behind the scenes and hold better conversations. I wasn’t trying to become a data scientist. I was trying to understand the system deeply enough to design it responsibly.

My relationship with Marcus, the Principal Data Scientist, started through the explainability work. A PM introduced us, Marcus showed me the existing screenshots and explained what he wanted, and I immediately asked to schedule one-on-ones with him because I knew I needed to understand this properly. Those sessions became one of the biggest reasons the project worked. Marcus would walk me through the workflow, explain concepts, answer endless questions, and sometimes literally co-design with me in real time while I moved things around in Sketch and asked him if I was understanding the logic correctly. I validated my understanding constantly because I was terrified of making something look nice while technically meaning the wrong thing. That would have been a fake win.

Those sessions weren’t just technical reviews. We talked about all kinds of things. Sometimes physics, sometimes books, sometimes charts, sometimes how a threshold slider changed the output of the model. Marcus loved machine learning. I loved user experience. That mutual passion mattered. He respected that I wasn’t treating him like a requirements machine and I think he could see how seriously I took the work. Over time, I went from asking basic questions to pushing back on his ideas when they no longer made UX sense. There was a point, over a year into the ML work, when he tried to bring Data Flow back into the experience in a way that contradicted all the decisions we had already made. By then, I had enough documentation, enough understanding, and enough confidence to say no. I reminded him of our prior decisions, explained why the change would hurt the workflow, and held my ground. I did the same on other topics too, like where certain controls belonged inside the IQ plugin. That was a big growth moment for me. I wasn’t just learning from the domain expert anymore. I could reason with the domain expert.

The actual redesign started to click once I realized the old workflow already had a spine. It was just buried. Training a model really came down to four core steps: choose the problem type, choose the target, choose the predictors, configure hyperparameters. That was it. That was the real shape of the task. Once I saw that, the design direction became much clearer. I tried several UI directions for expressing that structure. I explored full-screen layouts, side-step layouts, different orientations, different ways of showing all the steps at once. Eventually the modal workflow won. It fit Reporting Server better because popups were already native to how WebFOCUS behaved. It let me preserve the background context instead of making the whole world jump around. It gave me enough real estate for helper text, definitions, and inline guidance. It let me show one step at a time cleanly instead of overwhelming the screen. It was also easier for engineering to implement than a fancier full-screen dynamic flow. Sometimes the best design move is the one that solves the problem without asking the legacy product to become something it clearly is not.

That modal workflow was not about creating a beginner mode. We did not have two experiences. We had one path that served two purposes. I wanted something clear enough for people like me, who were new to ML, without making it feel dumb or insulting to the more technical users. That became a recurring tension with Marcus, who at one point basically reacted like, are you trying to make this dumb? My answer was no. I was not removing sophistication. I was making the sophistication legible. Every step had just enough explanatory text to help the user understand what they were deciding. Problem types had simple definitions. Target and predictor selection had contextual help. Hyperparameters had definitions and tooltips. The flow itself was obvious, but it wasn’t patronizing.

One of the biggest breakthroughs in the whole project was the landing page. Before the redesign, there was no meaningful Predict Data landing page at all. Users entered ML indirectly through Data Flow and Train Models. That made the whole feature feel buried. My instinct was immediate: why don’t we have a landing page here so users can choose data themselves, see what’s currently selected, and understand where they are? That idea was mine, and it kicked off a ton of iteration. At first I tried a combined Train and Run experience. Conceptually, that made sense to me because both activities belonged to the same mental space around the selected dataset. But the combined version got complicated fast. Engineering felt it was hard to support, and they also felt the stripped-down two-button approach I tried at one point was too much of a leap from what users were used to. So eventually I split Train and Run into two tabs on the same landing page.

That tab structure ended up being a good compromise. The Train tab explained what training meant, gave the user a clear CTA to train a new model, and showed models already trained on the selected dataset. If none existed, there was a proper empty state. The Run tab had the same general structure but focused on models available to run on that selected dataset. And the best part was that each model tile could include a play button, so running a model became basically one click. That felt huge. In the old system, running a trained model was a whole separate buried path. In the new design, it became visible and immediate.

Another major move was the right-click entry point. In WebFOCUS, right-click is like religion. Everything is right-clickable. Users are trained to expect important actions there. So putting Predict Data into the right-click menu of a master file was one of the smartest things I did on the project. It reduced the entry path from around six clicks to two. That alone changed discoverability. And once we saw how well that worked, we extended that pattern to other DSML-related actions too, like Generate Insights and Ask a Question. That wasn’t just a UX tweak. That was adoption thinking.

The model cards were another example of making decisions visible. Before, there really wasn’t a meaningful model display. You had file names. If you wanted details, you right-clicked, opened properties, read a panel, closed it, and repeated the whole process on another model. Some people suggested a table view, but that felt wrong to me. If users are choosing a model based on score, problem type, algorithm, and other properties, they shouldn’t have to inspect each one in isolation. They should be able to scan and compare. WebFOCUS already used tiles for files in the Hub, so I adapted that pattern. But these weren’t generic file tiles. I worked with the data scientist to figure out exactly what information mattered for decision-making and with my design director to make sure the UI still respected the design system. That led to a ton of iteration. The final cards surfaced the score, problem type, algorithm, trained dataset, and actions in a way that was much faster to evaluate.

The most complex screen in the whole project was probably the confusion matrix screen and, more broadly, the train model results architecture. The confusion matrix screen already existed in some form, but it was hard to understand. It had multiple charts, a threshold slider, and a matrix, but the relationship between them was not visually obvious. I had to work with Marcus in depth to understand what was actually changing when the threshold moved and how the layout could make that cause-and-effect visible. That is how I landed on things like aligning the threshold slider directly under the chart point that moved, simplifying the chart colors, and bringing the matrix into the main visual flow instead of leaving it buried at the bottom. That screen took at least ten iterations and a lot of logic-based debate, but it became one of the proudest moments of the project. Marcus publicly called it the best screen in the UX revamp during a DSML design walkthrough.

The results architecture was its own beast. Each problem type could have multiple algorithms, and each algorithm had multiple tabs. Binary classification in particular exploded into a huge number of views. What made it manageable in the end was not some magical trick. It was iteration, reducing visual clutter, cutting unnecessary clicks, and trying over and over until the hierarchy stopped feeling like a maze.

Error handling and guidance also got dramatically better in the new design. The old workflow barely handled failures at all. If a dataset was incompatible with a model, you got a vague warning after already trying it. Loading states just spun forever. Server errors were generic and useless. In the modal workflow, I finally had a place to bring in proper inline information, warnings, and errors based on the design system. That meant I could block invalid choices earlier, warn users before they retrained and lost results, and explain exactly what certain actions would do. In the optimize flow, for example, if the user changed predictors, we could warn them clearly that their current results would change and that they should save first if needed. If going back to a certain earlier step would effectively mean starting over, we could communicate that directly instead of letting them stumble into it.

Validation happened through multiple sessions with four SMEs, not five. That matters because I need to correct that in the case study. These were not pristine academic lab tests. This was enterprise reality. We showed ideas, ran separate sessions, and then let the SMEs walk through the flow. They already knew the old workflow, but the new one was obvious enough that they moved through it quickly with very little prompting. We mostly got refinement feedback on the results screens rather than major issues with the core four-step training flow. That told me a lot.

Working on ML at the same time as RC was controlled chaos. Two PMs, two engineering groups, QA, design system meetings, manager syncs, Slack trackers, JIRA, multiple morning meetings from six to nine almost every day. It was busy and intense, but I also kind of thrive in high-pressure environments. The bigger thing was that it forced me to become someone who could orchestrate across teams and understand how different parts of the product connected. Over time, I built a mental model of WebFOCUS as an end-to-end ecosystem: get data, prepare data, train and run ML, visualize in Designer, distribute through ReportCaster. Nobody explained that to me. I built that understanding by working across enough of the system.

The layoffs in 2025 didn’t really derail ML because ML was basically done by then. Design, development, and testing were practically complete. The open question was which release it would land in. What the layoffs did do was load more responsibility onto me across adjacent work, because other design work started falling back onto my plate. By then, though, ML was already in the final polish and cleanup stage. That should have felt satisfying. Instead, leaving before launch felt awful. Shitty, crappy, betrayed, like leaving my child and coming away. The work was ready. The delay was corporate machinery, red tape, restructuring, and all the usual nonsense that has nothing to do with the quality of the design.

What ML taught me, more than RC, was product partnership. With RC, I had to dig through chaos and make something presentable. With ML, I had a PM relationship that actually felt like co-ownership. My input shaped the roadmap, the concepts, and the direction. I worked deeply with Marcus, the PM, SMEs, engineers, and my design director. In hindsight, I was shaping the experience more than I even realized at the time. I only fully saw that after getting laid off and starting to build my portfolio case studies.

If I had to say what ML Functions means in my career story, it is pretty simple: if I can tackle something as complex as machine learning functions with no prior ML background and still make it usable, I can tackle anything. That is what this project proved to me. The biggest validation I missed by not seeing it fully launch was real usage data. I wanted adoption proof. I wanted to know whether the redesign actually helped get more users, retain users, or validate the design concepts I had fought so hard for. That evidence would have been powerful. But even without it, I know what I did.

The smartest decision in the whole project was probably putting Predict Data in the right-click menu and connecting it immediately to the landing page. Six clicks to two. Discoverability solved. The next smartest was the redesign of the trained model results area, especially around the confusion matrix and the optimize flow, because that is where users needed to understand, compare, and act intelligently.

There are still things I would improve if I had another pass. I would gray out incompatible models before users ever tried them. I would simplify the results screens even more. I would reduce context switching between training and running. There is always room to improve. But I do not feel like I left anything on the table in terms of effort. I pushed hard for what mattered. When technical constraints blocked something, that was technical reality, not lack of conviction on my part. I fought for every damn thing in ML.

If someone reads this case study and only understands one thing about me, I want it to be this: I do not back down. I do not need ideal conditions, ideal documentation, or prior domain expertise handed to me on a silver platter. If I set my mind to something, I will learn it, shape it, and deliver. ML forced me out of my comfort zone, and that pressure made me better. RC did too. In that sense, WebFOCUS gave me exactly the kind of growth I needed, even if it came wrapped in legacy pain and corporate chaos.

And honestly, when I think of ML now, I feel all of it at once. Pride. Sadness. Confidence. Unfinished business. Gratitude, weirdly enough. I think I needed WebFOCUS in my life. It taught me things I did not know before. It proved things to me that I might not have discovered anywhere else.

What a mind-bending project.
